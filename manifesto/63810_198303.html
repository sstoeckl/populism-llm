Economic Liberalism

Gemini · score 9.0 · verified · chunk 1
“destruction of Australian free enterprise”
This policy, if it were ever put into action, would put in place the essential framework for the creation of a Centrally Planned Economy - a State-controlled economy. ’There are other countries with Governments like this. In them, the unions are the puppets of self-appointed Governments. In Mr Hawke’s vision of Australia, the National Government would be the puppet of the union movement - and that movement, as Labor’s platform makes clear, would be outside the control of Parliament and the law, and responsible to no-one but itself. There are many aspects of the agreement between the ALP and the ACTU which I believe deserve the strongest condemnation. The worst is the whole philosophy of the agreement 7 a philosophy which provides for continually increasing wages, while grinding down profits, strangling growth and extending regulation. It is a philosophy which far from boosting jobs, would slash them, and that’s not surprising when you remember it comes with the blessing of the ACTU, whose wages and hours demands have already cost us thousand of jobs. But the most important thing about this policy document. and the one I want to make all Australians understand, is its basic thrust towards a centrally controlled and planned national economy. All the elements are there. Under Labor’s policies, there would be State control of prices, State control of incomes, State control of finance, State control of investment, and State control of interest rates. Over and above it all would be a national economic planning mechanism, an octopus stretching its tentacles into almost every aspect of economic activity. The emphasis of industry policy, the document itself makes plain, will be “to integrate all sectors both private and public. What an admission! This document makes it plain that a Labor Government would effectively throttle the private sector as we know it today. We would have an”integrated” economy whatever that means. Frankly, this policy document scares me. I believe it will scare all Australians who value their independence. It goes far beyond anything Mr Whitlam even attempted during his three disastrous years as Prime Minister. It’s what the radical wings of the ALP and the ACTU have been agitating for over many years. It would hand over control of the Government of this country to a union movement above Parliament. Socialism is an unfashionable word, I know. It’s trendy to scoff at those who warn that Labor is a party based on Socialist principles. Many Labor people try to pretend that isn’t true. But let’s look at the basic facts about the Labor Party. The Party Platform, determined only last year, states, and I quote: “The Australian Labor Party is a democratic socialist party and has the objective of the democratic socialisation of industry, production, distribution and exchange to the extent necessary to eliminate exploitation and other anti-social features of these fields”. The platform also calls for the redistribution of income, wealth, and economic power. It spells out the Labor enthusiasm for intervention in the economy, an enthusiasm echoed in the prices and incomes policy. And it names the instruments Labor is prepared to use to intervene in the economy - instruments which include nationalisation. It is clear from this document, the prices and incomes policy, that Labor’s commitment to the principles of Socialism is as strong as ever. It is a commitment which if ever put into action, would wreck the economy of this country. It is a commitment which would tear this country apart socially. And it is this commitment- spelt out in the prices and incomes policy- which sets out once and for all the absolute difference of Labor’s vision of our future and the vision of the Liberal-National Party Government. The last thing Australia needs is union government. Leaders must be strong, experienced, stable. We can handle our problems. Not easily, or quickly - but we can do it. We can do it if there’s leadership that’s strong and experienced. Leadership that’s respected - not necessarily loved, but respected - for its strength, its experience, its stability. We can do it if Australians accept the need for sound, safe, disciplined economic policies that continue to attack inflation. It’s not easy, but it’s the only way. When governments spend, you pay. Governments must do things to help. They must plan, construct, build for the future, to provide jobs and opportunities and challenges. The roads program. The water and soil conservation programs. The Territory railway, standard gauge links, mainline upgrading. A big airports program. The wage pause. The armed services job program for our young people and the forest and conservation jobs scheme. The general training programs for Australians, especially the youth of the nation. The help for industry. All these are part of our all-out effort to develop Australia and give Australians jobs. Australia will be what we make it. But in the end, it all gets back to us as individuals. Australia will be what we make it. I know Australians don’t talk much about things like pride in their country, about love of Australia. But they want to be proud. They want to see Australia grow and prosper. They want Australia to lye respected around the world. In many ways our name is respected except, that is, for our strikes. The Government must take the lead, and it is. But the Government alone can never solve all our problems. Australia isn’t just the Government. Australia is all of us. Australia will be what we all make it. With leadership built on experience and strength, we can make Australia a better place. That’s the kind of leadership Malcolm Fraser and I offer you. We ask for your help, your commitment, your work, to build a better Australia.
